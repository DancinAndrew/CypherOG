The **robot**, also called **mannequin** or **dancing machine**, is a [street dance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Street_dance "Street dance") style—often confused with [popping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popping "Popping")—that suggests the stilted movements of a dancing [robot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot "Robot") or [mannequin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mannequin "Mannequin"). Roboting gained fame in the 1970s after [Michael Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Jackson "Michael Jackson") used the dance when he performed "[Dancing Machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dancing_Machine "Dancing Machine")" with his brothers.<sup id="cite_ref-pop2_1-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-pop2-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>

The robot became popular in the 1980s, but goes back to the 1970s, when it was used theatrically in miming. It is a dance in which the dancer moves their limbs in a way that imitates the movements of a robot. Movements of the robot are _normally_ started and finished with a dimestop (a very abrupt stop), to give the impression of motors starting and stopping, but poppers have also been known to do the robot with a [pop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popping_(dance) "Popping (dance)") to the beat. As long as the illusion of being a robot is maintained, it is considered the robot.<sup>[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>

Robot dancing is often considered a subsection of popping because poppers often include the robot in their routines, sometimes adding pops to the beat while maintaining the illusion of a robot. However, the robot also exists as its own dance and is sometimes considered a performance rather than a dance when the performer is imitating a robot without any music. When done without music, it is considered to be [mime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mime "Mime"), instead of dance. Street theater often featured mimes who did a mechanical man or puppet style illusion, without music, including performer Robert Shields, who would later perform this style on a wider stage as part of [Shields and Yarnell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shields_and_Yarnell "Shields and Yarnell").<sup id="cite_ref-2"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In the late 1960s, the style was used while social dancing to funk or soul music. While not the first to imitate a robot as a mime, Charles "Robot" Washington<sup id="cite_ref-wash1_3-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-wash1-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and his partner "Robot Ann" were the first to socially couple dance the style to music at parties and clubs. It was at this point it became a party dance and later combined with other illusion styles to form today's popping style. It is commonly known as "Robotics". Roboting has also been likened to the jazz-era folk dance of puppeting (a style also appreciated in some colors of experimental ballet), whereby the dancer would emulate the mechanical movements of a simple musical box doll.<sup>[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup>

As with [popping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popping "Popping") in general, the visual impact of the robot can be boosted by doing it in pace with music. The best effect is achieved with music that has very distinct beats such as [electrofunk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electro_music "Electro music"). It is nonetheless common to use music not particularly suited for the dance, but which has a "robot theme". In 1969 [Jack Wild](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Wild "Jack Wild") did the robot in the "Mechanical Boy" episode of _[H.R. Pufnstuf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H.R._Pufnstuf "H.R. Pufnstuf")_. [The Jackson 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Jackson_5 "The Jackson 5") first performed "Dancing Machine" on _Soul Train_ on November 3, 1973,<sup id="cite_ref-4"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> where Michael Jackson performed a robot dance during the song's musical break. The group again performed the routine on _The Mike Douglas Show_ and the _Sonny and Cher Comedy Hour_ in 1974.<sup id="cite_ref-soultrain1_5-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-soultrain1-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-soul2_6-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-soul2-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup>

On September 29, 2009, the [Guinness World Record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guinness_World_Record "Guinness World Record") for the Largest Robot Dance was broken by [Robogals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robogals "Robogals") at the [University of Melbourne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Melbourne "University of Melbourne"), Australia. The previous record of 276 was broken when a Guinness World Record recognised 318 people were recorded as dancing in unison in approved robot style.<sup id="cite_ref-7"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_(dance)#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup>
