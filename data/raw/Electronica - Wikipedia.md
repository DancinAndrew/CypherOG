**Electronica** is both a broad group of [electronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_music "Electronic music")\-based music styles intended for listening rather than strictly for dancing<sup id="cite_ref-primer_2-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-primer-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-1"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and a music scene that came to prominence in the early 1990s in the United Kingdom which incorporated various musical styles.<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-2"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United States, the term is mostly used to refer to electronic music generally.<sup id="cite_ref-3"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup>

### Early 1990s: Origins and UK scene

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronica&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Early 1990s: Origins and UK scene")\]

The original widespread use of the term "electronica" derives from the influential English [experimental techno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_techno "Experimental techno") label New Electronica, which was one of the leading forces of the early 1990s introducing and supporting dance-based [electronic music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_music "Electronic music") oriented towards home listening rather than dance-floor play,<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-3"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> although the word "electronica" had already begun to be associated with [synthesizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesizer "Synthesizer") generated music as early as 1983, when a "UK Electronica Festival" was first held.<sup id="cite_ref-attritionrecollection_4-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-attritionrecollection-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-5"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-6"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> At that time electronica became known as "electronic listening music", also becoming more or less synonymous to [ambient techno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_techno "Ambient techno") and [intelligent techno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_techno "Intelligent techno"), and was considered distinct from other emerging genres such as [jungle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jungle_music "Jungle music") and [trip hop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trip_hop "Trip hop").<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-4"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>

Electronica artists that would later become commercially successful began to record in the late 1980s, before the term had come into common usage, including for example [the Prodigy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Prodigy "The Prodigy"), [Fatboy Slim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatboy_Slim "Fatboy Slim"), [Daft Punk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daft_Punk "Daft Punk"), [the Chemical Brothers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Chemical_Brothers "The Chemical Brothers"), [the Crystal Method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Crystal_Method "The Crystal Method"), [Moby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moby "Moby"), [Underworld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Underworld_(band) "Underworld (band)") and [Faithless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faithless "Faithless").<sup id="cite_ref-wired-method_7-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-wired-method-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup>

### Mid-1990s: Effect on mainstream popular music

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronica&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Mid-1990s: Effect on mainstream popular music")\]

Around the mid-1990s, with the success of the [big beat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_beat "Big beat")\-sound exemplified by the Chemical Brothers and the Prodigy in the UK, and spurred by the attention from mainstream artists, including [Madonna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna "Madonna") in her collaboration with [William Orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Orbit "William Orbit") on her album _[Ray of Light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_of_Light "Ray of Light")_<sup id="cite_ref-billboard1_8-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-billboard1-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> and Australian singer [Dannii Minogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dannii_Minogue "Dannii Minogue") with her 1997 album _[Girl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girl_(Dannii_Minogue_album) "Girl (Dannii Minogue album)")_,<sup id="cite_ref-9"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> music of this period began to be produced with a higher budget, increased technical quality, and with more layers than most other forms of [dance music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dance_music "Dance music"), since it was backed by major record labels and [MTV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MTV "MTV") as the "next big thing".<sup id="cite_ref-technoculture2_10-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-technoculture2-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>

According to a 1997 _[Billboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Billboard_(magazine) "Billboard (magazine)")_ article, "the union of the club community and [independent labels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_record_label "Independent record label")" provided the experimental and trend-setting environment in which electronica acts developed and eventually reached the mainstream. It cites American labels such as [Astralwerks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astralwerks "Astralwerks") (the Chemical Brothers, Fatboy Slim, [the Future Sound of London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Future_Sound_of_London "The Future Sound of London"), [Fluke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluke_(band) "Fluke (band)")), [Moonshine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonshine_Music "Moonshine Music") ([DJ Keoki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DJ_Keoki "DJ Keoki")), [Sims](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sims_Records "Sims Records"), and City of Angels (the Crystal Method) for playing a significant role in discovering and marketing artists who became popularized in the electronica scene.<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_11-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-billboard-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup>

Madonna and [Björk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bj%C3%B6rk "Björk") are said<sup>[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual_of_Style/Words_to_watch#Unsupported_attributions" title="Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch"><span title="The material near this tag may use weasel words or too-vague attribution. (August 2016)">by whom?</span></a></i>]</sup> to be responsible for electronica's thrust into mainstream culture, with their albums _Ray of Light_ (Madonna),<sup id="cite_ref-billboard1_8-1"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-billboard1-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> _[Post](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post_(Bj%C3%B6rk_album) "Post (Björk album)")_ and _[Homogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogenic "Homogenic")_ (Björk).

### Late 1990s: American inclusion

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronica&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Late 1990s: American inclusion")\]

In 1997, the North American mainstream music industry adopted and to some extent manufactured _electronica_ as an umbrella term encompassing styles such as [techno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Techno "Techno"), big beat, [drum and bass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drum_and_bass "Drum and bass"), trip hop, [downtempo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Downtempo "Downtempo"), and [ambient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_music "Ambient music"), regardless of whether it was curated by indie labels catering to the "underground" nightclub and [rave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rave "Rave") scenes,<sup id="cite_ref-billboard_11-1"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-billboard-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-MIT_12-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-MIT-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> or licensed by major labels and marketed to mainstream audiences as a commercially viable alternative to [alternative rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_rock "Alternative rock") music.<sup id="cite_ref-NYmag_13-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-NYmag-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup>

[New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") became one center of experimentation and growth for the electronica sound, with DJs and music producers from areas as diverse as [Southeast Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southeast_Asia "Southeast Asia") and Brazil bringing their creative work to the nightclubs of that city.<sup id="cite_ref-latin1_14-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-latin1-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-youth1_15-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-youth1-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup>

## Characteristics and definition

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronica&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Characteristics and definition")\]

Electronica benefited from industry advancements in [music technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_technology "Music technology"), especially [electronic musical instruments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_musical_instruments "Electronic musical instruments"), synthesizers, [music sequencers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_sequencer "Music sequencer"), [drum machines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drum_machine "Drum machine"), and [digital audio workstations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_audio_workstation "Digital audio workstation"). As the technology developed, it became possible for individuals or smaller groups to produce electronic songs and recordings in smaller studios, even in [project studios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recording_studio#Project_studios "Recording studio"). At the same time, computers facilitated the use of music [samples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampling_(music) "Sampling (music)") and [loops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(music) "Loop (music)") as construction kits for sonic compositions.<sup id="cite_ref-direct2_16-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-direct2-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> This led to a period of creative experimentation and the development of new forms, some of which became known as electronica.<sup id="cite_ref-holmes5_17-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-holmes5-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-wired-BT_18-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-wired-BT-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Wide ranges of influences, both sonic and compositional, are combined in electronica recordings.<sup id="cite_ref-technoculture1_19-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-technoculture1-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup>

Electronica includes a wide variety of musical acts and styles, linked by a penchant for overtly electronic production;<sup id="cite_ref-direct1_20-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-direct1-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> a range which includes more popular acts such as Björk, Madonna, [Goldfrapp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldfrapp "Goldfrapp") and [IDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intelligent_dance_music "Intelligent dance music") artists such as [Autechre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autechre "Autechre"), and [Aphex Twin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aphex_Twin "Aphex Twin").

### Regional differences

\[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Electronica&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Regional differences")\]

The North American mainstream music industry uses the term as an umbrella category to refer any dance-based electronic music styles with a potential for [pop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_music "Pop music") appeal.<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-5"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> However, United States–based [AllMusic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AllMusic "AllMusic") still categorizes electronica as a top-level genre, stating that it includes danceable [grooves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groove_(music) "Groove (music)"), as well as music for headphones and [chillout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chill-out_music "Chill-out music") areas.<sup id="cite_ref-AMG_21-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-AMG-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup>

In other parts of the world, especially in the UK, electronica is also a broad term, but is associated with non-dance-oriented music, including relatively [experimental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_music "Experimental music") styles of listening electronic music. It partly overlaps what is known chiefly outside the UK as intelligent dance music (IDM).<sup id="cite_ref-bogdanov_1-6"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-bogdanov-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>

In the late 1990s and early 2000s, electronica was increasingly used as background scores for [television advertisements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Television_advertisement "Television advertisement"), initially for automobiles. It was also used for various video games, including [the _Wipeout_ series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wipeout_(video_game_series) "Wipeout (video game series)"), for which the soundtrack was composed of many popular electronica tracks that helped create more interest in this type of music<sup id="cite_ref-taylor_22-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-taylor-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup>—and later for other technological and business products such as computers and financial services.<sup>[<i><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2016)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Then in 2011, [Hyundai Veloster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyundai_Veloster "Hyundai Veloster"), in association with [the Grammys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Grammys "The Grammys"), produced a project that became known as Re:Generation.<sup id="cite_ref-RE:GENERATION_23-0"><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronica#cite_note-RE:GENERATION-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup>
